# Abstract

Power system simulation and electricity-market optimization underpin the planning and operation of modern grids, yet the dominant open-source framework for this domain, PyPSA, faces a performance limitation: for large networks and iterative scenario studies its Python-based model-assembly layer can dominate the total runtime, no self-contained Julia reimplementation with a PyPSA-compatible interface exists, and the integration of a calibrated demand forecast into a stochastic dispatch within a single native framework remains largely unexplored. This thesis reimplements PyPSA's core computational modules in Julia and augments them with an AI-assisted forecasting layer. Five solver modules — DC power flow, AC power flow (via PowerModels.jl and Ipopt), linearized AC power flow, single- and multi-period Linear Optimal Power Flow with storage and wind generation, and Unit Commitment as a mixed-integer linear program — are implemented and validated against PyPSA on a 3-bus network and the IEEE 14-bus case; for the linear-programming methods the Julia and PyPSA results are numerically identical, and the remaining differences are explained by documented per-unit conventions. Because both implementations call the same HiGHS and Ipopt solvers, the measured speedup is isolated to the compiled model-assembly layer; benchmarks across networks from 3 to 2,000 buses show speedups ranging from about 7x for AC power flow to over 750x for large Linear Optimal Power Flow problems. An LSTM load forecaster equipped with distribution-free conformal prediction intervals supplies demand scenarios to a sample-average-approximation stochastic dispatch, which reports expected cost and conditional value-at-risk — risk measures that a deterministic formulation cannot provide. The complete implementation is released as the PowerFlowJulia package.

**Keywords:** power system optimization, Julia, PyPSA, optimal power flow, unit commitment, load forecasting, conformal prediction, stochastic optimization, locational marginal prices.
